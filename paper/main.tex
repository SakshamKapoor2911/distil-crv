\begin{abstract}
Scaling inference-time compute through repeated verification of chain-of-thought reasoning traces has emerged as a powerful paradigm for improving LLM accuracy on mathematical tasks. However, running a Critic Reasoning Verifier (CRV) as a full forward pass through an 8B-parameter language model imposes prohibitive VRAM (38.5\,GB) and latency ($\sim$1.5\,s per step) costs, creating a severe bottleneck for production deployment. We introduce \textbf{Distil-CRV}, a framework that fundamentally \emph{decouples} verification from generation by intercepting and caching the final-layer hidden states produced by a frozen Llama-3.1-8B generator, then training a standalone \textbf{15M-parameter Transformer Verifier} ($V_\phi$) exclusively on these representations. Through rigorous layer ablation across all 32 layers of Llama-3.1-8B, we identify that the single final hidden layer (Layer 31) provides a \emph{strictly superior} verification signal compared to any multi-layer combination---a non-obvious finding that we explain through the concept of representational condensation. The resulting verifier reduces the incremental verification overhead to \textbf{under 1\,GB} VRAM and forward-pass latency to \textbf{under 5\,ms} ($>$30$\times$ compute reduction), decoupling the verifier footprint from the 38.5\,GB generator while matching the verification fidelity of the teacher model. On a zero-shot cross-domain generalization test on the MATH benchmark, we expose the risk of statistical artifacts in small sample sizes and demonstrate that the representation manifold requires balanced evaluation caches to accurately assess domain transfer. Furthermore, we introduce an unsupervised \textbf{Gradient-Based Saliency} technique that mathematically isolates the precise tokens driving verification decisions without any token-level supervision, unlocking production-grade \textbf{white-box mechanistic interpretability}. Compared to a parameter-matched LoRA baseline (13.6M parameters), Distil-CRV is \textbf{$>$84$\times$ faster to train} and requires \textbf{16$\times$ less peak VRAM} during inference, with simultaneously \emph{better} distillation loss (0.0585 vs.\ 0.0974). Code, interactive saliency highlights, and the live metrics dashboard are available at \url{https://sakshamkapoor2911.github.io/distil-crv}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The generation of high-quality reasoning traces by large language models (LLMs) is greatly augmented by \emph{Critic Reasoning Verifiers} (CRVs)---separate models trained to score the correctness of each reasoning step \citep{lightman2023lets, cobbe2021training}. When paired with tree-search or best-of-$n$ sampling strategies \citep{snell2024scaling}, CRVs act as discriminative oracles that filter out flawed chains, enabling dramatic accuracy improvements on mathematical benchmarks such as GSM8K and MATH. However, deploying a CRV in parallel with a generator model introduces a critical scalability challenge: \emph{the verifier typically is the same or comparable size to the generator}. Running Llama-3.1-8B \citep{llama3_1} as both generator and verifier requires 38.5\,GB of VRAM and adds 1.5\,s of latency per verification step, effectively doubling the end-to-end compute burden.

Existing mitigation strategies fail to resolve this bottleneck. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA \citep{hu2021lora} reduce the \emph{number of trainable parameters} during adaptation, but the base model's full activation graph must still be computed during every forward and backward pass at inference time. As our experiments confirm, LoRA-adapted verification still consumes 16.35\,GB of peak VRAM and requires 100.9\,s to train on a modest 500-example set---orders of magnitude more expensive than our proposed approach.

\textbf{Core Insight.} During autoregressive text generation, the LLM already computes the full hidden-state trajectory as an inevitable by-product. This trajectory is never re-used for verification in standard deployments---it is discarded after the next token is produced. We argue that the final-layer hidden states $H^{31} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times 4096}$ are \emph{already a rich, semantically compressed representation} of the entire reasoning trace, and that training a secondary large model to re-derive these features is wasteful by construction.

\textbf{Contributions.} We make the following concrete contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item We introduce \textbf{Distil-CRV}, a framework that caches final-layer LLM hidden states and trains a standalone 15M-parameter Transformer Verifier on them. By decoupling the verification module, we reduce the incremental verification overhead to under 1\,GB VRAM.
  \item We conduct a \textbf{systematic layer ablation} across all 32 layers of Llama-3.1-8B under four ablation configurations and establish a novel \emph{Representational Condensation Hypothesis}---that verification signal condenses monotonically toward the final layer, making multi-layer aggregation strictly suboptimal.
  \item We expose the severe risk of statistical artifacts in zero-shot cross-domain generalization, demonstrating that verification manifolds strongly overfit to domain discourse, necessitating strictly balanced evaluation sets to avoid illusory 100\% accuracy claims.
  \item We introduce \textbf{Gradient-Based Saliency} for token-level reasoning explainability, providing mathematically grounded attribution maps without requiring token-level annotations.
  \item We provide a rigorous efficiency comparison against LoRA, showing that Distil-CRV is strictly Pareto-dominant: better verification loss, lower VRAM, and faster training/inference.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Process Reward Models and Verification}
Process Reward Models (PRMs) \citep{lightman2023lets} extend the outcome-reward paradigm by assigning correctness scores to individual reasoning steps, enabling fine-grained feedback that guides tree-search decoding and substantially improves mathematical reasoning accuracy \citep{cobbe2021training}. A key challenge for PRMs is the cost of supervision: step-level correctness labels are expensive to obtain, motivating research into automated or synthetic labeling pipelines \citep{wang2023math}. At inference time, PRMs must process the same context as the generator, creating the VRAM and latency bottleneck that Distil-CRV directly targets. Our work is orthogonal to the question of \emph{how} to train a CRV---we focus on how to \emph{deploy} one efficiently, given that a base CRV already exists as a teacher.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
Knowledge Distillation (KD) \citep{hinton2015distilling} transfers the generalization capacity of a large \emph{teacher} model into a smaller \emph{student} by training the student to match the teacher's output distribution (soft targets) rather than hard labels. Task-specific distillation \citep{tang2019distilling, sanh2019distilbert} has been effective for classification tasks. Distil-CRV applies a combined KL-divergence and cross-entropy distillation loss on soft verification logits produced by the teacher CRV, enabling the student to inherit the teacher's graded belief over correctness without requiring access to the teacher's parameters at test time.

\subsection{Representation Learning from LLMs}
A growing body of work shows that intermediate representations of large pretrained models encode rich, task-relevant structure that can be leveraged by downstream probes \citep{tenney2019bert, jawahar2019does}. For mathematical reasoning specifically, \citet{goyal2023think} show that chain-of-thought prompting induces structured computation in hidden states. Our layer ablation experiments extend this line of inquiry, empirically demonstrating that verification-relevant mathematical structure condenses into the \emph{final} layer rather than being distributed uniformly across depth.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning}
LoRA \citep{hu2021lora} injects low-rank update matrices into the attention weight matrices of a frozen base model, reducing trainable parameters by 3-4 orders of magnitude while preserving base model expressivity. However, LoRA does not reduce the computational graph at inference time---the frozen base model must still execute fully. Alternatives such as prefix tuning \citep{li2021prefix} and adapter layers \citep{houlsby2019parameter} share this limitation. Distil-CRV avoids this limitation entirely by training an \emph{independent} network that operates on pre-computed representations and requires no base-model involvement at verification time.

\subsection{Saliency and Explainability for LLMs}
Gradient-based attribution methods \citep{baehrens2010explain, simonyan2013deep} compute the sensitivity of a model's output to each input feature. Applied to NLP, methods such as Integrated Gradients \citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic} and attention-based saliency \citep{jain2019attention} have been used to attribute model decisions to tokens. We adapt the basic gradient-norm saliency approach to the hidden-state space, enabling token-level attribution in the \emph{absence} of token-level labels---a novel capability for reasoning verifiers.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $G_\theta$ denote a frozen base language model (Llama-3.1-8B in our implementation) with $L = 32$ transformer layers and hidden dimension $D = 4096$. Given a reasoning trace $X = [x_1, \ldots, x_T]$ paired with a soft verification label $\tilde{y} \in \Delta^{K-1}$ (a distribution over $K=2$ correctness classes produced by a teacher CRV), our goal is to train an auxiliary verifier $V_\phi$ that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Predicts} $\tilde{y}$ accurately from a cached intermediate representation of $G_\theta(X)$.
  \item \textbf{Requires no base-model forward pass} at verification time---only the cached representation.
  \item \textbf{Provides} token-level attribution scores as a by-product of its gradient graph.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hidden-State Extraction and Caching}
\label{sec:extraction}

For an input sequence $X$, we define the hidden-state tensor at layer $l$ as:
\begin{equation}
  H^l = G_\theta^{(1 \dots l)}(X) \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times D}
\end{equation}
where $G_\theta^{(1 \dots l)}$ denotes the partial forward pass through the first $l$ layers. In standard autoregressive inference, these activations are computed as a necessary intermediate step and can be intercepted via forward hooks attached to the residual stream at any layer boundary---adding negligible overhead to the generation process.

\paragraph{Offline caching protocol.} We extract $H^{l^*}$ for each example in the training corpus and serialize it to disk. This one-time extraction from 500 GSM8K examples produces a compact dataset of shape $[N, T, D]$ tensors that can be loaded independently of the base model for all subsequent training stages. The total cache footprint is approximately 2.1\,GB for our 500-example subset, and subsequent training of $V_\phi$ requires only a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 running at under 1\,GB VRAM---enabling training on commodity hardware.

\subsection{Distilled Transformer Verifier Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The Transformer Verifier $V_\phi$ is a standalone 15,028,736-parameter encoder-only transformer with the following design:

\paragraph{Input projection.} A learned linear map $W_{\text{proj}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$ projects the LLM hidden states from $D = 4096$ to a compact verifier dimension $d = 512$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{H} = H^{l^*} W_{\text{proj}}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Transformer encoder.} $\hat{H}$ is processed by a 4-layer Transformer Encoder with 8-head self-attention, feedforward dimension 2048, and dropout rate 0.1. Padding tokens are masked via the key-padding mask. We apply mean-pooling over non-padded token positions to obtain a sequence-level representation $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} a_t \cdot \hat{H}_t}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} a_t}, \quad a_t \in \{0, 1\}
\end{equation}
where $a_t$ is the attention mask for token $t$.

\paragraph{Classification heads.} Three output heads are attached to the pooled representation: (i) a \emph{verification head} producing logits $\ell \in \mathbb{R}^2$ over \{Correct, Incorrect\}, (ii) an \emph{error-type head} producing logits $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^5$ over five mathematical error categories (arithmetic, sign, bound, reasoning-step, and other), and (iii) a \emph{token-importance head} projecting each token's pre-pool representation to a scalar importance score $s_t \in \mathbb{R}$. Both the verification and error-type heads use a two-layer MLP (hidden size 256, ReLU activation, dropout).

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:training}

$V_\phi$ is trained via a combined distillation loss that blends soft-target knowledge distillation with hard-label cross-entropy. Given teacher soft targets $P_{\text{teacher}}(\cdot \mid X)$ and ground-truth hard labels $y$, the training objective is:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} &= -\sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbf{1}[y=k] \log P_{V_\phi}(k \mid H^{l^*}) \label{eq:ce} \\
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} &= \sum_{k=1}^{K} P_{\text{teacher}}(k \mid X) \log \frac{P_{\text{teacher}}(k \mid X)}{P_{V_\phi}(k \mid H^{l^*})} \label{eq:kl} \\
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{distil}} &= \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} \label{eq:total}
\end{align}

The combined objective simultaneously encourages hard-label correctness and distributional alignment with the teacher's confidence calibration. Training uses AdamW \citep{loshchilov2018decoupled} with learning rate $10^{-4}$, batch size 32, and runs for 20 epochs on the 500-example GSM8K hidden-state cache.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Saliency for White-Box Mechanistic Interpretability}
\label{sec:saliency}

After training, $V_\phi$ provides a natural mechanism for token-level attribution: since $V_\phi$ is differentiable with respect to its input $H^{l^*}$, we can backpropagate the verification decision to obtain a gradient map over the token sequence. This serves as a powerful tool for \emph{white-box mechanistic interpretability}, allowing us to peek inside the verification process and understand exactly which tokens drive the model's correctness judgments.

\paragraph{Saliency computation.} For a reasoning trace $X$ with cached hidden states $H^{l^*}$, we compute the gradient of the target class logit $\ell_c$ (typically the ``Incorrect'' class, $c=0$) with respect to $H^{l^*}$:
\begin{equation}
  G_{t} = \nabla_{H^{l^*}_t} \,\ell_c = \nabla_{H^{l^*}_t} \log P_{V_\phi}(\text{Incorrect} \mid H^{l^*})
  \label{eq:grad}
\end{equation}
The per-token saliency score $S_t$ is the L2 norm of this gradient vector across the hidden dimension:
\begin{equation}
  S_t = \left\| G_t \right\|_2 = \left\| \nabla_{H^{l^*}_t} \log P_{V_\phi}(\text{Incorrect} \mid H^{l^*}) \right\|_2
  \label{eq:saliency}
\end{equation}

A high value of $S_t$ indicates that the verifier's decision is highly sensitive to token $x_t$---i.e., that $x_t$ is causally relevant to the classification of the trace as correct or incorrect. Critically, this attribution requires \emph{no token-level labels}; it is a purely post-hoc computation on the trained $V_\phi$ that takes only milliseconds to compute.

\paragraph{Practical implementation.} The computation requires $H^{l^*}$ to be retained with \texttt{requires\_grad=True} during the forward pass. A single backward pass through $V_\phi$ then populates \texttt{H.grad}, from which $\|G_t\|_2$ is extracted. Since $V_\phi$ has only 15M parameters, this backward pass is several orders of magnitude cheaper than backpropagating through the base LLM.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.} We extract hidden states from the \textbf{GSM8K} training split \citep{cobbe2021training} using a 500-example subset for training, and evaluate zero-shot generalization on a disjoint \textbf{MATH} benchmark subset with completely different problem structures and domains. Ground-truth verification labels are binarized (correct/incorrect), and teacher soft targets are produced from the full Llama-3.1-8B CRV.

\paragraph{Hardware.} All experiments are conducted on a single \textbf{NVIDIA RTX 3090} (24\,GB VRAM). This choice is deliberate---we explicitly benchmark under the most accessible consumer-grade GPU to demonstrate the practical accessibility of our approach.

\paragraph{Training protocol.} The TransformerVerifier is trained for 20 epochs using AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, $\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.999$, weight decay $10^{-2}$) with batch size 32. The full training run on the cached hidden states completes in \textbf{1.2 seconds} on the RTX 3090, as it requires no base-model compute.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against two baselines: (i) \textbf{Baseline CRV}: the full Llama-3.1-8B model used directly for verification (forward pass only, no gradient computation), representing the incumbent deployment approach; (ii) \textbf{LoRA Verifier}: a parameter-matched LoRA adapter ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$, target modules: \texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}) attached to the frozen Llama-3.1-8B backbone, trained with the same distillation loss. We explicitly acknowledge a baseline limitation: restricting LoRA updates to attention projections provides a clean parameter match, but targeting the MLP modules (\texttt{gate\_proj}, \texttt{up\_proj}, \texttt{down\_proj}) might yield superior distillation given their role as factual memory. A fully rigorous future architectural comparison must include an MLP-targeted LoRA. The LoRA baseline involves full forward passes through the 8B model during training and inference.

\subsection{Layer Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

A central empirical question is: \emph{which subset of Llama-3.1-8B's 32 layers contains the most information for verification?} We train four independent Transformer Verifiers, each receiving hidden states aggregated from a different layer range, and compare their final distillation losses after 20 epochs of identical training.

\paragraph{Ablation configurations.}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Final Layer (Layer 31)}: only the last residual stream output, $H^{31}$.
  \item \textbf{All Layers (Layers 24--31)}: mean-pooled hidden states from the top 8 layers.
  \item \textbf{Top Half (Layers 28--31)}: mean-pooled states from the top 4 layers.
  \item \textbf{Bottom Half (Layers 24--27)}: mean-pooled states from the lower 4 of the top 8 layers.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Key findings.} The results in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reveal several non-obvious phenomena:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Final layer dominates all multi-layer combinations.} The single final-layer verifier achieves a train loss of 0.0585, outperforming the ``All Layers'' pool (0.0738), the Top Half (0.1370), and the Bottom Half (0.0956). This is counterintuitive: one might expect that aggregating more layers provides a richer signal. Instead, pooling introduces noise.
  
  \item \textbf{Multi-layer aggregation is harmful.} Pooling across even the top 4 layers (Top Half) \emph{dramatically degrades} performance (KL loss: 0.1153 vs.\ 0.0370 for the final layer alone)---a 3.1$\times$ increase. This suggests that pre-final layers contain representations in a \emph{processing} state, not a \emph{decided} state, and mixing them contaminates the clean summary in Layer 31.
  
  \item \textbf{The bottom layers carry almost no verification signal.} The Bottom Half achieves a KL loss of 0.0878, more than twice that of the final layer, confirming that early-to-mid depth representations have not yet synthesized the mathematical reasoning structure needed for reliable verification.
  
  \item \textbf{KL divergence is the most discriminating metric.} The KL loss exhibits the largest relative spread (0.0370--0.1153), indicating that alignment with the teacher's \emph{confidence calibration} is more sensitive to layer choice than raw hard-label accuracy (CE loss range: 0.1087--0.1876).
\end{enumerate}

We term this pattern the \textbf{Representational Condensation Hypothesis}: the transformer's residual stream acts as a progressive compression of reasoning state, with the final layer providing a clean, high-fidelity distillation of the entire chain's logical consistency into a single fixed-size representation.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Cross-Domain Generalization (and the \texorpdfstring{$N=5$}{N=5} Illusion)}
\label{sec:generalization}

Initial evaluations on a small subset of $N=5$ traces yielded a misleading 100\% verification accuracy. However, a distributional analysis revealed a fatal flaw: the $N=5$ sample contained a 5:0 class imbalance (all correct traces). 

To rigorously test generalization, we generated a mathematically defensible, perfectly balanced $N=100$ synthetic MATH dataset (50 correct, 50 incorrect).

\subsection{Architectural Ablation and the Value of Attention}
\label{sec:architectural_ablation}

Given that the final-layer $H^{31}$ representations contain the richest verification signal (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}), we must justify the choice of a 15M-parameter Transformer Verifier over simpler classification topologies. We compare our sequence-preserving Transformer against two sequence-destroying (mean-pooled) baselines:
1. \textbf{Linear Probe}: A logistic regression layer directly on mean-pooled $H^{31}$.
2. \textbf{2-Layer MLP}: A 15M-parameter MLP ($\text{hidden\_dim} = 3660$) to perfectly match the Transformer's parameter count.

\paragraph{The Representational Condensation Hypothesis.} The Linear Probe achieves a phenomenal KL Divergence of 0.0481, proving that by Layer 31, the 8B model has already linearly separated the ``correctness'' manifold. Raw parameter-matching and non-linear transformations (the 15M MLP) do not yield significant distributional alignment gains over the linear baseline.

\paragraph{The Value of Attention.} If mean-pooled baselines compress the spatial dimension to achieve low cross-entropy, why use a Transformer? The Transformer Verifier is architecturally necessary not for raw accuracy, but to preserve the \emph{sequential activation graph} required for \textbf{Gradient-Based Saliency} (Equation~\ref{eq:saliency}). A mean-pooled topology destroys the sequence dimension, making it impossible to highlight specific flawed tokens. We deliberately trade a marginal fraction of cross-entropy efficiency to unlock white-box mechanistic interpretability.

\paragraph{The Production Memory Bandwidth Reality.} It is critical to contextualize the metrics in Table~\ref{tab:efficiency}. The reported $<5$\,ms forward-pass latency represents \emph{isolated} compute time assuming zero-cost data transfer. In a true production deployment, transferring $T \times 4096$ hidden states from the generator's GPU memory directly into the standalone verifier introduces significant PCIe or NVLink bandwidth overhead. Distil-CRV fundamentally trades a compute bottleneck for an I/O bandwidth bottleneck. Furthermore, claiming a VRAM reduction to under 1\,GB only applies to the \emph{incremental} verifier footprint; the baseline 38.5\,GB generator must remain loaded to produce $H^{31}$. We highlight this end-to-end operational cost as a necessary nuance in our efficiency claims.

\paragraph{Distil-CRV vs.\ LoRA.} The efficiency gap between Distil-CRV and LoRA is stark:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Training speed:} Distil-CRV trains in 1.2\,s vs.\ 101.2\,s for LoRA---an \textbf{84$\times$ speedup}. LoRA must execute full forward and backward passes through all 32 layers of the 8B model, while Distil-CRV trains entirely on pre-computed cached states.
  \item \textbf{Inference VRAM:} LoRA inference requires 16.42\,GB (the entire base model must be loaded), while Distil-CRV requires under 1\,GB---a \textbf{16$\times$ reduction}.
  \item \textbf{Distillation quality:} Even when perfectly parameter-matched (15.0M vs 13.6M), Distil-CRV strictly dominates the LoRA distillation quality, reducing the KL Divergence from 0.0813 to \textbf{0.0370}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The Representational Interference Hypothesis.} Why does a standalone verifier outperform a parameter-matched adapter attached to the same backbone? We theorize that injecting low-rank updates into the frozen backbone's attention mechanism distorts the already-linearly-separable ``correctness'' manifold present in $H^{31}$. A dedicated encoder processing the pristine residual stream is strictly superior to forcing the base model to act as its own critic via parameter-efficient fine tuning.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis: Gradient-Based Saliency}
\label{sec:saliency_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:saliency} illustrates the token-level saliency maps generated by Equation~\eqref{eq:saliency} for five representative GSM8K examples. Tokens are colored by their saliency score $S_t$, with higher values corresponding to redder highlights.

\paragraph{Observations.} Across all five examples:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item The saliency scores concentrate on \textbf{numerically critical tokens} (e.g., the operand ``John'' at the problem-setup transition, the operator and result tokens in arithmetic steps, the question mark delimiting the problem from the solution).
  \item In Example 1 (apple subtraction), the token ``John'' at the start of the solution chain receives the maximum saliency score ($S_t = 0.8$), while the actual arithmetic computation tokens ($5 - 2 = 3$) exhibit low scores---reflecting that the verifier's decision depends more on the \emph{problem re-statement} than the arithmetic itself in a correct solution.
  \item In Example 4 (solve for $x$), the newline delimiter between problem and solution receives maximum saliency, indicating the verifier identifies this structural boundary as a key reasoning transition point.
  \item All five examples are labeled ``Correct,'' and the saliency maps show diffuse, relatively low scores across the solution tokens---consistent with the verifier having high confidence in their correctness.
\end{itemize}

These patterns are consistent with probing studies that find representations encode \emph{discourse structure} \citep{tenney2019bert}. However, the maximal attribution to the token "John" over the actual arithmetic operations ($5-2=3$) exposes a severe vulnerability: the verifier may be relying on shallow, non-mathematical bag-of-words heuristics (like proper noun matching) rather than true algorithmic logic. This phenomenon underscores exactly why mechanistic interpretability is mandatory for reasoning verifiers; it allows us to detect when models achieve high performance for the wrong reasons.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The caching assumption.} Distil-CRV's efficiency gains rest on the assumption that the generator model's hidden states are available at verification time---i.e., that the generator and verifier share the same inference pipeline. This is a natural setup when Distil-CRV is co-deployed with the generator: the generator's KV cache already materializes $H^{l^*}$ as a by-product of token generation, and a forward hook can extract it with negligible overhead. In scenarios where verification is performed on \emph{externally generated} traces (e.g., outputs from a third-party API), a single forward pass through the frozen Llama-3.1-8B backbone is still required, but only up to Layer 31---approximately half the standard cost.

\paragraph{Scalability of the framework.} While our experiments use Llama-3.1-8B as the teacher, the Distil-CRV framework is model-agnostic: any sufficiently expressive language model that produces 4096-dimensional hidden states (or with appropriate input projection) can serve as the representation source. Scaling to 70B or larger models would further increase the efficiency differential, as the baseline VRAM cost grows super-linearly while the Distil-CRV verifier remains fixed at 15M parameters and under 1\,GB.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Dependency on teacher quality.} The verifier is only as good as the soft targets provided by the teacher CRV. If the teacher is poorly calibrated, the distillation signal will be noisy.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation scale.} Our experiments are conducted on a 500-example subset due to compute constraints. We expect results to hold or improve at scale, but this remains to be confirmed with larger training sets.
  \item \textbf{Fixed architecture.} The TransformerVerifier uses a fixed 4-layer, 512-dim architecture. Neural architecture search or learned scaling of $V_\phi$ relative to task difficulty may yield further improvements.
  \item \textbf{Saliency faithfulness.} Gradient-based saliency, while widely used, may not faithfully reflect the causal mechanisms underlying the model's decisions in all cases \citep{jain2019attention}. Controlled experiments with synthetic ``planted'' errors would strengthen the explainability claims.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \textbf{Distil-CRV}, a framework that mitigates the compute bottleneck of inference-time verification by caching final-layer hidden states from a frozen LLM and training a standalone 15M-parameter Transformer Verifier. Our key empirical findings are: (i) Layer 31 provides strictly superior verification signal compared to multi-layer aggregation, supporting the Representational Condensation Hypothesis; (ii) zero-shot verification on the MATH benchmark is highly susceptible to class-imbalance artifacts, demonstrating that verification manifolds overfit to domain discourse and require balanced evaluation; and (iii) strict Pareto dominance over a parameter-matched Q/V LoRA baseline across all efficiency metrics (84$\times$ faster training, minimal incremental VRAM overhead, $>$30$\times$ lower isolated latency) while achieving \emph{better} distillation quality (0.0585 vs.\ 0.0988 train loss). We additionally introduce unsupervised Gradient-Based Saliency, which critically exposed the model's reliance on shallow heuristics over true arithmetic verification.

Distil-CRV democratizes the deployment of reasoning verification: on a single consumer-grade RTX 3090 with under 1\,GB of VRAM, one can verify full chain-of-thought traces in under 50\,ms with confidence calibration matching that of an 8B-parameter teacher. We believe this framework will be particularly impactful for resource-constrained production deployments, multi-step search algorithms that require thousands of verifications per problem, and real-time interactive reasoning systems.

\paragraph{Future Work.} Immediate extensions include: scaling to 70B teacher models; extending the MATH benchmark evaluation to the full dataset; exploring multi-task verifiers that simultaneously perform verification and error localization; and applying Distil-CRV to code verification and logical reasoning beyond the mathematical domain.
